Between Two Worlds:
The Hybrid Struggle

A collection of essays on the historical and contemporary struggles of mixed-heritage peoples — with particular focus on the Black-Asian experience in America.

Essay I

Neither / Nor: The Double Exile of the Mixed-Race Body

On belonging nowhere and the specific exhaustion of perpetual explanation.

✦
Aisha St. Claire-Webb
Director of Advocacy, BBBC · Published March 2026

There is a particular kind of loneliness that belongs only to those who exist between categories. Not the loneliness of isolation — of having no one around you — but the loneliness of being surrounded by people who are all, in some fundamental way, not quite you. It is the loneliness of the room where everyone else seems to have arrived already knowing where they stand, and you are still, quietly, trying to figure out which wall to lean against.

This is the condition of the mixed-race individual in America. It is not a new condition — it predates the republic itself — but it is one that has been systematically ignored, romanticized, pathologized, and erased in roughly equal measure, depending on the era and the convenience of those doing the categorizing.

The Architecture of Belonging

American racial identity was never designed to accommodate complexity. The one-drop rule — the legal and social principle that any traceable African ancestry made a person Black, regardless of appearance, culture, or self-identification — was not a biological observation. It was a property law. It existed to ensure that the children of enslaved women and their enslavers remained enslaved, that wealth did not travel across the color line through inheritance, and that the brutal hierarchy of American society could be maintained with maximum efficiency.

The one-drop rule was only one instrument in a much larger apparatus. The broader project of American racial categorization — codified in census classifications, antimiscegenation statutes, naturalization law, and school segregation policies — was always fundamentally about sorting people into boxes that served the interests of those who built the boxes. People of mixed heritage have never fit cleanly into those boxes. And the system's response to that failure of fit has never been to question the boxes. It has always been to punish the people.

"To be mixed in America is to be a living refutation of a lie the country has told about itself for four hundred years. The country does not thank you for this."

— Aisha St. Claire-Webb

The Specific Weight of Explanation

Ask any person of mixed heritage about the texture of their daily life and you will hear, eventually, about the question. Not a question — the question. "What are you?" It arrives in checkout lines and classrooms, at job interviews and dinner parties, from strangers and from people who have known you for years. It is asked with curiosity, with suspicion, with the unmistakable subtext of a system that needs you to declare yourself before it can decide how to treat you.

The exhaustion of this is not merely social. It is cognitive, emotional, and cumulative. Every mixed-race person carries an internal archive of answers, calibrated to context, to safety, to the particular racial anxieties of whoever is asking. You learn early that the wrong answer — or more precisely, an answer that fails to satisfy — produces a follow-up. "But what are you really?" The question behind the question: pick a side. The system needs you legible. Your complexity is an inconvenience.

This pressure to choose — to simplify, to perform monoracial identity for the comfort of others — is one of the foundational injuries of the mixed-race experience. It is a slow, ambient demand that you diminish yourself. And it comes not just from white America, but from within communities of color, where identity policing can be just as fierce, and where the stakes of perceived inauthenticity carry their own particular weight.

The Double Exile

The mixed-race person often finds themselves subject to suspicion from multiple directions simultaneously. Too light to be fully trusted in one community, too dark to be fully welcome in another. Not Black enough. Not Asian enough. Not white enough. The goalposts move depending on who is watching and what they need you to be. The result is a form of social homelessness — a chronic condition of partial belonging that is rarely named and almost never addressed in the mainstream discourse on race in America.

What the BBBC understands, and what this collection of essays attempts to document, is that this experience is not peripheral. It is not an edge case or a footnote. It is the experience of tens of millions of Americans, and it is getting more common with every generation. The question is not whether mixed-heritage identity will become a central feature of American racial life. It already is. The question is whether American institutions — legal, political, cultural — will evolve fast enough to stop treating complexity as a problem to be solved rather than a reality to be honored.

Essay II

Between Black and Asian: A Hybrid History in America

On the particular struggles of those who carry both inheritances — and the communities that have not always known what to do with them.

✦
Marcus Obi-Chen
Defense Corps Sergeant, Detroit HQ · Contributing Writer

The Black-Asian mixed-race experience in America is among the most under-examined and least understood of all hybrid identities, despite being one of the oldest. It is older than the term "multiracial." It is older than most of the institutions that now struggle to categorize it. It stretches back to the 19th century, when Black and Asian laborers worked alongside one another in the mines and on the railroads of the American West, lived in the same boarding houses, ate in the same restaurants — the ones that would have them — and sometimes, inevitably, built families together.

Those families existed largely in the shadows. They were illegal in many states, invisible in the census, and unwelcome in the mainstream of both the Black and Asian communities of the era, each of which was fighting its own desperate battle for survival and social standing in a country that wanted neither of them.

Shared Ground, Separate Struggles

The history of Black-Asian contact in America is inseparable from the history of American labor exploitation. Both communities were brought to or confined within the country as sources of cheap, expendable labor. Chinese workers built the transcontinental railroad under conditions of near-slavery. Black Americans were enslaved, and then, after Emancipation, were systematically excluded from the economic mobility that the railroad and the industrial economy were supposed to make possible.

In cities like San Francisco, Los Angeles, and New York, Black and Asian communities occupied overlapping urban geographies — not because of natural affinity, but because they were both excluded from white neighborhoods through restrictive covenants, redlining, and the blunt instrument of racial terror. This shared exclusion created proximity, and proximity created relationships. It created, over generations, children.

"My grandmother was Chinese and my grandfather was Black. They met in a laundry on Stockton Street in San Francisco in 1923. The state of California said their marriage was illegal. They got married anyway. I am what happened when they refused to obey." — BBBC member testimony, Annual Summit 2024

The Post-War Generation and the Korean and Vietnam Conflicts

A significant wave of Black-Asian mixed-race Americans emerged from the aftermath of the Korean and Vietnam Wars. Black soldiers — serving in a military that had only been formally desegregated in 1948 and was still riven with institutional racism — formed relationships with Korean, Vietnamese, Filipino, and Japanese women in the countries where they were stationed. The children of those relationships occupied a uniquely difficult position: too Black for Asian societies that prized ethnic purity, and too Asian — or simply too foreign — for the Black American communities their fathers returned to.

Many of these children, known in South Korea as honhyeol and in Vietnam as con lai, faced extraordinary discrimination in their birth countries. Those who came to the United States found a different but equally inhospitable landscape. They were exotic to white America, suspect to Black America, and invisible to Asian America. The word for what they were — mixed, hybrid, blended — had no positive valence in any of the cultures that had produced them.

"To be Black and Asian in America is to carry two histories of exclusion in a single body — and to be told, by both, that you have not suffered enough to belong."

— Marcus Obi-Chen, BBBC Contributing Writer

Contemporary Pressures: The Model Minority and the Stereotype's Shadow

The "model minority" myth — the stereotype of Asian Americans as universally educated, economically successful, and politically compliant — has caused particular harm to Black-Asian mixed-race individuals. It operates as a wedge, positioning Asian Americans against Black Americans in a manufactured competition for social legitimacy, and it disappears the enormous diversity of the Asian American experience, including the very existence of Black-Asian hybrid people.

For Black-Asian individuals, the model minority myth creates an impossible double standard. When they succeed academically or professionally, it is attributed to their Asian heritage. When they struggle, or when they are subjected to the racism that Black Americans face systemically, they are told — implicitly or explicitly — that their Asian side should have protected them. The result is a kind of identity math in which neither half adds up to a whole, and the person is perpetually in deficit.

Solidarity and Its Limits

The spring of 2020 and the COVID-19 pandemic produced two simultaneous crises of racial violence in America: the murder of George Floyd and the explosion of anti-Asian hate crimes. Both communities were targeted. Both communities organized. And for a brief, fragile moment, there was public conversation about Black-Asian solidarity — a recognition that the two communities shared common enemies in white supremacy and the racial scapegoating politics of the American right.

That solidarity, real and important as it was, also exposed fault lines. There were incidents of anti-Asian violence perpetrated by Black individuals, and incidents of anti-Black racism within Asian American communities — fault lines that a history of manufactured competition and mutual misrepresentation had made almost inevitable. For Black-Asian mixed-race Americans, these moments were particularly painful. They were caught between communities that were simultaneously reaching toward each other and pulling apart, asked to pick a side in a conflict they embodied the transcendence of.

The BBBC's position is not that these fault lines do not exist. They do. But the existence of Black-Asian hybrid people — millions of them, living proof that these communities have always been more intertwined than the political discourse allows — is itself a form of solidarity. A genealogical fact that no amount of manufactured division can fully erase.

Essay III

The Law Against Us: Antimiscegenation, Identity Erasure, and the Courts

A legal history of how American law has worked to prevent, punish, and erase mixed-race people and their families.

✦
Sofia Nguyen-Brooks
Legislative Correspondent, BBBC · J.D., Howard University School of Law

The history of mixed-race people in America is, to a significant degree, a history of legal persecution. From the earliest colonial antimiscegenation statutes of the 17th century to the racial classification systems that persist in federal bureaucracy today, American law has consistently worked to prevent the existence of hybrid people, to punish those who came into existence anyway, and to erase the evidence of their lives from the official record.

Understanding this history is not merely an academic exercise. It is the foundation of the BBBC's legislative agenda, and it is essential context for understanding why the struggles of mixed-race people — including Black-Asian hybrid individuals — are not simply personal or cultural, but structural and political.

The Colonial Origins: Slavery and the Fear of Mixture

The first antimiscegenation laws in North America were enacted in Virginia in 1691, prohibiting marriage between white colonists and Black, mulatto, or Native American people. These laws were not primarily motivated by racial disgust, though that was certainly present. They were motivated by property. Mixed-race children of enslaved women and free men were a problem for the system of hereditary slavery: they complicated inheritance, blurred the legal distinctions on which slavery depended, and — most dangerously — produced free people of color who could not be easily re-enslaved.

The solution was legislative brutality. Mixed-race children of enslaved mothers were enslaved regardless of their fathers' status. Mixed-race marriages were criminalized. The free movement and civil standing of mixed-race people were progressively restricted. By the time of the Civil War, virtually every slave state had statutes specifically targeting mixed-race individuals, their families, and their property rights.

The Exclusion Acts and Asian Hybridity

The legal persecution of Black-Asian hybrid families operated through two parallel legal frameworks. The antimiscegenation laws targeting Black Americans were joined, from the 1870s onward, by a series of federal exclusion acts targeting Asian immigrants — particularly Chinese, Japanese, Filipino, Korean, and South Asian people — that progressively restricted Asian immigration, naturalization, land ownership, and intermarriage with white Americans.

The Cable Act of 1922 stripped American women of their citizenship if they married men ineligible for citizenship — a category that included virtually all Asian men. The Alien Land Laws, enacted across multiple Western states, prohibited Asian immigrants from owning agricultural land. The internment of Japanese Americans during World War II — explicitly justified on racial grounds — demonstrated the extent to which Asian Americans could be treated as enemy bodies regardless of citizenship, length of residence, or demonstrated loyalty.

"The law did not simply tolerate the persecution of mixed-race families. The law was the persecution." — Sofia Nguyen-Brooks

Loving v. Virginia and Its Limits

The Supreme Court's 1967 decision in Loving v. Virginia, which struck down all remaining state antimiscegenation laws as unconstitutional, is rightly celebrated as a landmark civil rights victory. It made legal the marriages that had existed illegally — or in exile, or in secret — for generations. It acknowledged, at the highest level of American jurisprudence, that the right to marry across racial lines was a fundamental right that no state could abrogate.

What Loving did not do was address the structural conditions that made mixed-race families vulnerable in ways that extended far beyond the marriage certificate. It did not create legal recognition of mixed-race identity. It did not reform the census classifications that continued to force hybrid individuals into monoracial boxes. It did not address the housing discrimination, employment discrimination, educational discrimination, and social exclusion that mixed-race families continued to face. It removed one weapon from the arsenal of anti-hybrid persecution while leaving the arsenal largely intact.

The Present: Documentation, Classification, and Invisibility

Today's legal landscape for mixed-race Americans is one of incomplete recognition and structural invisibility. The 2000 census, for the first time, allowed respondents to select more than one racial category — a reform that the BBBC and its predecessor organizations fought for across decades. But that reform has not been fully implemented across federal agencies, many of which continue to operate under legacy classification systems that do not accommodate hybrid identity.

In employment law, the legal protections against racial discrimination have been inconsistently applied to discrimination targeting people specifically because of their mixed-race status — a form of discrimination that is distinct from discrimination against monoracial people and that requires specific legal recognition. The BBBC's 2026 legislative push for the Multiracial Recognition Act is aimed directly at this gap: the distance between the legal acknowledgment that mixed-race people exist and the structural accommodation of that existence in American law.

Essay IV

Colorism and the Internal War: How Communities Wound Their Own

On the discrimination mixed-race people face not from white supremacy alone but from within the communities that should be their shelter.

✦
Priya Osei-Williams
Legislative Advocate, New York Chapter · BBBC Senior Fellow

The cruelest injuries are often those inflicted by people who love you. Or who are supposed to. For mixed-race individuals navigating Black and Asian communities simultaneously, the wounds inflicted from within — the accusations of inauthenticity, the colorist hierarchies, the suspicion of divided loyalty — can be as disabling as any discrimination from the outside. And they are far less discussed, because discussing them requires confronting uncomfortable truths about the ways that communities of color have internalized the racial logics of the systems that oppress them.

Colorism in the Black Community

Colorism in the Black community — the preference for lighter skin tones and the discrimination against darker ones — is a direct inheritance of slavery. The plantation system assigned different roles to enslaved people based on the proximity of their skin color to whiteness: lighter-skinned enslaved people were more likely to work in the house, to receive limited educational access, to be granted small privileges that created a hierarchy within the enslaved community itself. This was not an accident. It was policy, designed to fracture Black solidarity along the color line and to make the maintenance of racial hierarchy an internal as well as external function.

For Black-Asian mixed-race individuals, colorism operates in complex and sometimes contradictory ways. Lighter skin may confer certain social privileges within Black spaces while simultaneously marking the person as suspect — as not fully committed, as ambiguously loyal, as someone whose lighter complexion signals potential inauthenticity. The term "lightskin" carries both aspiration and accusation, depending on who uses it and in what context.

"Colorism is white supremacy with the serial numbers filed off. It does the same work of division and hierarchy using the same logic of skin, just operated from the inside."

— Priya Osei-Williams

Ethnic Purity and the Asian Family

Within many Asian communities, the pressure toward ethnic endogamy — marriage within one's ethnic group — is intense and multigenerational. This pressure varies significantly across different Asian ethnic communities, but it is present, in some form, across most of them. It is reinforced by family structure, by cultural memory of colonial disruption, and by the specific ways in which Asian American communities have navigated American racism: through the cultivation of cultural distinctiveness as a form of self-preservation.

For Black-Asian mixed-race individuals, this can manifest as explicit rejection. Grandparents who refuse to acknowledge mixed grandchildren. Family gatherings where the hybrid member is made to feel like a guest. Cultural events and traditions from which mixed-heritage people are implicitly excluded. The message, rarely stated directly, is that their existence represents a dilution — a failure of ethnic continuity — rather than an expansion of what the community is and can be.

The specific additional burden for Black-Asian individuals is the anti-Blackness that exists within some Asian communities — a prejudice that, again, is partly an inheritance of American racial hierarchy, which positioned proximity to whiteness as the path to social mobility and Blackness as the furthest pole from that aspiration. This anti-Blackness is not universal, and many Asian American communities have worked consciously to confront and dismantle it. But its presence means that the Black-Asian mixed-race person may face not just the generic suspicion of the hybrid, but the specific hostility directed at Blackness, from within their own extended family.

The BBBC Position on Internal Discrimination

The Brotherhood of Blended Bloodlines Committee does not approach colorism and internal community discrimination as subjects that are too sensitive to name. We name them because silence serves the wound. We name them because the mixed-race individual who is rejected by the communities that partly constitute them deserves to have that experience acknowledged and analyzed, not euphemized into invisibility.

At the same time, the BBBC does not approach these communities as enemies. The Black community and the various Asian American communities are not our adversaries. They are our families. The work of confronting colorism and ethnic exclusionism within those communities is work that many people within those communities are already doing, and doing courageously. Our role is to advocate for the specific interests of hybrid people within that broader project — to insist that the future those communities are building must have room for us, as we are, without subtraction.

Essay V

Reclaiming the Hybrid: Identity as Resistance

On the radical act of refusing simplification — and the political, cultural, and personal work of building hybrid identity on our own terms.

✦
Joshua A.C. Wyand
Chairman & Founder, BBBC

I want to tell you something that took me forty years to fully believe: there is nothing wrong with you. Not the part that does not fit the census form. Not the part that confuses people at parties. Not the part that gets asked the question. Not the part that carries two histories simultaneously and sometimes does not know how to hold them both at once. There is nothing wrong with any of it. The problem has never been you. The problem is the boxes. And the boxes can be broken.

Hybridity as Political Fact

Mixed-race identity is not just a personal reality. It is a political one. Every person of blended heritage who refuses to check a single box, who insists on the full complexity of their identity in a context designed to demand simplification, is performing a political act. Not because they have chosen to be political, but because the system has made their existence a political question. When the government asks you to declare a single racial identity and you say "I won't — I am more than one thing," you are refusing the premise of a system that needs you to be legible in a particular way in order to process you efficiently. That refusal matters.

The BBBC was founded on the conviction that this refusal should be organized, resourced, and sustained — that the scattered, individual acts of identity resistance performed by millions of mixed-race Americans every day should be coordinated into a political force capable of changing law, policy, and culture. That is what we are building. And it is working.

The Cultural Work of Hybrid Identity

Alongside the political work, there is cultural work. The project of building a positive, self-determined hybrid identity — one that does not define itself primarily by what it is excluded from but by what it is — is ongoing and unfinished. It requires art, literature, music, oral history, and the kinds of community gathering that the Annual Summit represents: spaces where hybrid people can be in the presence of other hybrid people and experience what it feels like to be the norm rather than the exception.

For Black-Asian mixed-race individuals specifically, this cultural work involves the recovery of a history that has been largely lost. The stories of the 19th-century families in San Francisco's Chinatown and Fillmore District. The children of soldiers and the women of Korea and Vietnam. The families that were illegal and the families that survived their illegality. These stories exist. They are in archives, in family photographs, in the memories of elders who are still living. They deserve to be told and preserved — not as curiosities, but as the foundation of an identity that is continuous, rooted, and real.

"We did not begin as a problem to be solved. We began as love — the specific, defiant love of people who refused to let the law tell them who they could belong to."

— Joshua A.C. Wyand, Chairman

To the Young and Blended

This final section is for the young people. For the teenager who does not know which lunch table to sit at. For the college student who gets the question from the diversity committee. For the child who is the only one who looks like them in every room they enter.

You are not a problem. You are not a compromise. You are not half of anything. You are the full product of every person who came before you — all of their cultures, all of their histories, all of their survival and their love. The fact that the world has not yet built adequate categories for you is not your failure. It is the world's incomplete project, and it is one that organizations like the BBBC exist to advance.

Your identity is not something that needs to be resolved. It is not a question waiting for an answer. It is an answer — to the centuries of law and custom and violence that tried to prevent you from existing. You exist. You are here. You are exactly what you are. And that is enough. It has always been enough.

The Brotherhood of Blended Bloodlines Committee was built for you.

Historical Record

A Timeline of Hybrid Struggle in America

Key moments in the legal, political, and social history of mixed-race people in the United States.

✦
1691
Virginia enacts the first colonial antimiscegenation statute, criminalizing marriage between white colonists and Black or Native American people. Penalties include banishment for white women who marry Black men.
1790
The Naturalization Act limits citizenship to "free white persons," establishing race as a precondition for belonging in the new republic and setting the framework for the legal exclusion of mixed-race people from full civic standing.
1850s
Large-scale Chinese immigration to California for Gold Rush labor brings Black and Asian workers into proximity in mining camps and urban neighborhoods. Informal communities and families begin to form in cities like San Francisco.
1882
The Chinese Exclusion Act bans Chinese laborers from entering the United States and bars Chinese immigrants from naturalization. This and subsequent Asian Exclusion Acts effectively criminalize Asian-white and Asian-Black family formation.
1896
Plessy v. Ferguson establishes the "separate but equal" doctrine, entrenching racial segregation in law. Homer Plessy himself was mixed-race — seven-eighths white — demonstrating the one-drop rule's reach and the law's indifference to hybrid identity.
1922
The Cable Act strips American women of citizenship if they marry men ineligible for citizenship — targeting Asian men specifically. American women who marry Asian men lose their nationality, and their children's citizenship is placed in jeopardy.
1942
Executive Order 9066 authorizes the mass internment of Japanese Americans. Over 120,000 people — nearly two-thirds of them American citizens — are imprisoned. Families of mixed Japanese-Black or Japanese-other heritage face unique legal ambiguity about their status.
1948
The U.S. military is formally desegregated by Executive Order 9981. Black soldiers begin serving alongside white and Asian soldiers in Korea and Japan, creating conditions for Black-Asian family formation that produces a new generation of mixed-race Americans.
1967
Loving v. Virginia strikes down all remaining antimiscegenation laws as unconstitutional. Richard and Mildred Loving — a white man and a Black-Native American woman — become the named plaintiffs in the case that ends the legal prohibition on interracial marriage.
1970s–80s
A generation of children born to Black servicemen and Vietnamese, Korean, and Filipino women begin arriving in the United States, many facing rejection from both their birth countries and their fathers' communities. Mixed-race advocacy organizations begin to form in response.
1997
After decades of advocacy from multiracial organizations, the Office of Management and Budget revises its racial classification standards to allow individuals to identify with more than one race — paving the way for the 2000 census to include multiracial options for the first time.
2000
The U.S. Census Bureau allows respondents to select more than one race for the first time. Nearly 7 million Americans — 2.4% of the population — identify as multiracial. The true number is believed to be significantly higher due to social pressure toward monoracial identification.
2009
The Brotherhood of Blended Bloodlines Committee is founded in Detroit, Michigan by Joshua A.C. Wyand and a coalition of multiracial families, civil rights attorneys, and community organizers committed to political advocacy for hybrid and mixed-heritage peoples.
2011
⚔ Defense Corps Founded. The BBBC Defense Corps is established as the organization's community protection wing, formed in response to a wave of racially motivated hate crimes targeting mixed-race families in Detroit's Midtown corridor. Sixteen founding members — veterans, martial artists, and community security volunteers — complete the Corps' first six-week training program.
2013
⚔ Operation Bloodline Shield — Chicago. Defense Corps units deploy to Chicago's South Side after vandals firebomb a mixed-race family center affiliated with the BBBC. Corps members establish 72-hour protective patrols, escort members to and from the facility, and coordinate with local law enforcement. No further incidents occur during the four-week operation.
2016
⚔ Corps Expansion — 12 Cities. Following a recruitment drive at BBBC's national convention, Defense Corps chapters are formally chartered in Atlanta, Houston, Los Angeles, Philadelphia, Minneapolis, Seattle, Baltimore, Miami, Denver, Phoenix, St. Louis, and Detroit HQ. Each chapter adopts a standardized training protocol and chain of command under Corps Commander Damon O. Ashford.
2017
⚔ Joint Training with Veterans' Organizations. The Defense Corps conducts its first joint field exercises with the Multiracial Veterans' Alliance, a network of mixed-heritage former service members. The exercises — held at a private training facility outside Columbus, Ohio — include land navigation, conflict de-escalation, and community emergency response protocols.
2020
The murders of George Floyd and Breonna Taylor and the explosion of anti-Asian hate crimes during the COVID-19 pandemic simultaneously mobilize Black and Asian communities. ⚔ Defense Corps mobilizes across all 12 chapters, providing armed escorts for BBBC members, protecting chapter offices from vandalism, and coordinating mutual aid networks that deliver food and medicine to mixed-race households targeted during unrest. A national conversation begins — imperfectly, incompletely — about Black-Asian solidarity and the shared enemies of both communities.
2022
⚔ Operation Iron Helix — Baltimore. Corps intelligence identifies a coordinated harassment campaign targeting eight multiracial families in the Park Heights neighborhood. Defense Corps units establish a residential protection perimeter lasting eleven weeks. Three suspects are apprehended by local police following Corps surveillance documentation. Families report zero further incidents following the operation's conclusion.
2024
⚔ Hybrid Heritage Security Network Launched. The Defense Corps launches a decentralized community watch program — the HHSN — spanning 34 cities. Members receive encrypted communication tools, emergency response training, and direct lines to BBBC legal counsel. The network responds to over 200 reported bias incidents in its first year, providing documentation and support that results in 47 hate crime charges filed by local prosecutors.
2026
The BBBC delivers testimony before the Senate Subcommittee on Civil Rights in support of the Multiracial Recognition Act, pressing for formal federal recognition of mixed-heritage identity across all government data collection systems. ⚔ Defense Corps Command simultaneously files a formal brief documenting 847 verified hate incidents against mixed-race individuals across 28 states between 2020 and 2025 — the most comprehensive record of anti-hybrid violence ever submitted to a federal body. The struggle continues.
